The Imagination Gap

By Mitchell Hillman — April 23, 2026

Artificial intelligence is not just making software faster to build. It is changing what developers think to build in the first place.

The cost of a software idea has always been paid in time. Building a working application from scratch demands weeks of skilled labour at minimum, often months. Most ideas die in the gap between conception and execution, not because they are bad but because they are too expensive. Yet artificial intelligence is collapsing that cost with unexpected speed. When effort drops sharply enough, imagination changes.

I have spent fifteen years building web applications in JavaScript. That is long enough to develop a good intuition for what shipping software costs in terms of time. When I recently used Claude to build a native MacOS photo-editing application, I had no background in native development. An AI assistant handled the architecture and scaffolding. The application worked without any handwritten code contributions by myself. With only hours of tinkering, the app was feature complete including white-balance controls and bulk file-export functionality. The limit, I realised, was no longer how much time I had. It was my imagination.

This effect represents the hidden tax on software creativity. Developers habitually pre-filter ideas by estimated effort; any project too large for available hours gets shelved before it begins. The filter is so automatic it rarely registers as a constraint. AI does not eliminate it entirely, but it lowers the threshold substantially.

The practical consequences are concrete. Consider a second case: visualising a large dataset as a graphic artwork. In my case, I wanted to produce historic timelines based on large CSV datasets. A programmatic approach is technically superior to manual work in a design application, but only if the time spent writing the supporting software is lower than that of drawing by hand in graphic software. For a solo developer working evenings, those costs have been close to equal, neutralising the advantage. With AI assistance handling the interface and scaffolding code, the programmatic approach becomes decisively cheaper. A new category of solution becomes economically viable.

The same logic extends to analytical tools, internal utilities and experimental products that previously fell below the viability threshold.

For companies, the implications run deeper than productivity. Engineering talent has long been among the primary differentiators in the technology industry. If execution becomes cheaper, the scarce resource shifts toward conception: the ability to define what should be built, for whom, and why. Product judgment, design sensibility and domain expertise appreciate in value as raw coding capacity cheapens.

This is not without risk. Speed of execution can outrun quality of conception. Software built quickly and without friction is still software that can fail, alienate users or solve the wrong problem elegantly. The hard problems have not disappeared; they have relocated. When I say that working with AI allows me to “imagine a delightful experience” instead of a minimum viable product, I am describing access to a harder problem, not an easier one.

The imagination gap is closing. Whether wisdom closes with it remains to be seen.

Mitchell Hillman is a software engineer living and working in Charlottesville, Virginia.
