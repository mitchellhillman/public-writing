The most productive meeting in my week is the refinement meeting. It is also, invariably, the worst part of my day. The hopes and dreams of software that might change people's lives get trampled by a parade of pedantic pageantry, replete with ritual and ceremony. "Hey! Will you vote?" calls the PMP or CSM. "I could go down to a 3," mutters the listless engineer. The yoke of office work never feels so heavy as during an agile planning session. But don't reach for your pitchfork yet.

Many pundits on the internet declare Agile dead, killed by AI as all disruptions lately seem to be. Steve Jones, an executive vice president at Capgemini, argues that AI agents building software in hours have made the Manifesto's human-centric principles obsolete, with "agentic SDLCs simply too fast for Agile." Yet Forrester's 2025 research finds that 95% of professionals still affirm Agile's critical relevance, and 61% report using it for five or more years. Agile methodology is a common vocabulary that enables, however imperfectly, the collaboration that future success depends on. Rolf Läderach, an operational excellence coach, writes that "Agile is about creating adaptive and learning organisations that can respond to change and deliver outcomes. That need will never disappear. And AI supports this." The Agile Business Consortium goes further, finding that mature agility practices are a prerequisite for effective AI integration. Teams rushing to adopt AI on top of dysfunctional Agile foundations are simply powdering their wigs.

The gap between adoption and maturity is the real problem. Google's research on team effectiveness shows that what distinguishes teams from working groups is interdependence, and that what makes teams effective is less about who is on the team than how they work together. Yet most organisations use Agile in name only. Only 13% have it deeply embedded, and 42% describe their approach as "better than nothing but could be more effective," according to Digital.ai's 18th Annual State of Agile Report. The British Computer Society finds that only 16% of organisations achieve high Agile maturity despite near-universal adoption. Jeff Gothelf, one of the original Lean UX thinkers, puts it in four words. Saturation does not equal maturation.

The ceremonies that frustrate practitioners most reveal the same pattern of form without function. A 2026 survey of 419 teams found that 80% experience significant sprint rollover, with between a quarter and half of planned work left incomplete each cycle. Only 31% use collaborative estimation techniques, while 37% estimate solo or asynchronously, bypassing the alignment discussion that is the entire point of the exercise. And 69% of teams implement fewer than 60% of the action items from their retrospectives.
Waterfall offers no rescue. Agile projects are three times more likely to succeed than waterfall-based ones, according to the Standish Group's 2020 Chaos Study. The migration is already underway, with 74% of organisations shifted to hybrid or custom frameworks rather than reverting to sequential delivery. Waterfall does not scale to large projects with overlapping teams and constantly reforming rosters. The coordination problem Agile was designed to solve has not gone away.

Short of a Jacobin revolution, then, here are incremental reforms worth adopting.

**For practitioners**

- Norm on Fibonacci numbers. They are not perfect but they are better than S, M, L sizing, where somehow every story is a Medium. Even if the points are filed away and ignored after refinement, the exercise of alignment is the real output.
- Either track velocity or stop reporting sprint output in numbers. What gets measured gets managed, and in Fibonacci everything drifts toward a 5. Low estimates feel risky for developers and earn no recognition. High estimates alarm managers. The clustering pressure makes the numbers meaningless.
- Replace sprints with a prioritized backlog. No developer has ever genuinely cared about sprint boundaries. They encourage underestimating to avoid the embarrassment of overruns, and unplanned work gets silently added mid-cycle with no credit given. A prioritized backlog gives overachievers visibility into the full scope of the project, gives steady contributors a clear signal ("pick from the top"), and gives managers a meaningful progress bar to share with stakeholders.
- Try asynchronous story review before the meeting. Teams that read tickets in advance arrive with questions rather than first impressions, which makes the estimation discussion sharper and shorter.

**For managers**

- Do not ask the team to pick its own name. You answer to your stakeholders and the team answers to you. Decide on a theme and assign names. Include the team in your vision. Do not shirk that responsibility.
- Keep retrospectives small and safe. Large cross-team retros become status meetings where only the most senior developers are willing to offer criticism. Retros require psychological safety, and that often means the manager cannot be in the room. But if the manager is absent, nothing gets fixed. The solution is many small retros, attended by managers, with concrete follow-up items tracked in the backlog immediately afterward.
- Shield the team from project anxiety. Most engineers are focused on the technical domain in front of them. Reporting daily managerial challenges to the team raises stress without improving output.

The Agile ancien regime is in genuine need of reform, but a reign of terror is sure to follow those who would abolish it entirely.
