--- layout: post title: "The Imagination Gap" byline: "Mitchell Hillman" date: 2026-04-23 image: /img/square-circle.jpg standfirst: "Artificial intelligence is changing what developers imagine building, and the effect runs deeper than speed alone." ---

The cost of a software idea has always been paid in time. Building a working application from scratch demands weeks of skilled labour at minimum, often months. Most ideas die in the gap between conception and execution because the cost of building them is simply too high. Yet artificial intelligence is collapsing that cost with unexpected speed. When effort drops sharply enough, imagination changes.

I have spent fifteen years building web applications in JavaScript, long enough to have a calibrated sense of what software costs in time. A feature-complete photo editor, built from scratch in an unfamiliar environment, would normally take months. Using Claude, it took a few hours. I had no background in native MacOS development. An AI assistant generated the architecture, the scaffolding, and the structure. The white-balance controls and bulk file-export were working before I had written a single line of code. The limit, I realised, had become my imagination.

This effect represents the hidden tax on software creativity. Developers habitually pre-filter ideas by estimated effort. Any project too large for available hours gets shelved before it begins. The filter is so automatic it rarely registers as a constraint. AI does not eliminate it entirely, but it lowers the threshold substantially.

The practical consequences are concrete. Take, for instance, visualising a large dataset as a graphic artwork. In my case, I wanted to produce historic timelines based on large CSV datasets. A programmatic approach is technically superior to manual work in a design application, but only if the time spent writing the supporting software is lower than that of drawing by hand in graphic software. For a solo developer working evenings, those costs have been close to equal, neutralising the advantage. With AI assistance handling the interface and scaffolding code, the programmatic approach becomes decisively cheaper. A new category of solution becomes economically viable.

The same logic extends to analytical tools, internal utilities and experimental products that previously fell below the viability threshold.

For companies, the implications run deeper than productivity. Engineering talent has long been among the primary differentiators in the technology industry. If execution becomes cheaper, the scarce resource shifts toward conception. The ability to define what should be built, for whom, and why becomes the scarce asset. Product judgment, design sensibility and domain expertise appreciate in value as raw coding capacity cheapens.

The shift carries real risks. When execution becomes cheap, speed can outrun the quality of conception, and software built quickly and without friction is still software that can fail, alienate users or solve the wrong problem elegantly. The hard problems have relocated. When I say that working with AI allows me to “imagine a delightful experience” instead of a minimum viable product, I am describing access to a harder problem.

The imagination gap is closing. Whether wisdom closes with it remains to be seen.
